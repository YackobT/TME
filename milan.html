Milan

Architectural Icons

The Duomo di Milano took nearly six centuries to build, beginning in 1386 and finishing in the 20th century. Its façade blends Gothic, Renaissance and Neoclassical elements, crowned by forest of spires, flying buttresses and gargoyles. Covering 126,000 square feet, the cathedral can hold up to 40,000 worshippers and features 3,159 statues, including the gilded Madonnina atop the main spire.

Shopping & Symbolism

Adjacent to the cathedral, the Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II is a 19th‑century shopping arcade enclosed by a soaring glass and iron roof. Designed by Giuseppe Mengoni and built between 1865 and 1867, the gallery connects Piazza del Duomo to Piazza della Scala through a cruciform passage crowned by a 47 metre dome. Patriotic mosaics on the floor depict the coats of arms of Italian cities; stepping on the bull of Turin is believed to bring good luck.

Masterpiece of the Renaissance

Milan is home to Leonardo da Vinci’s Last Supper. Painted between 1494 and 1498 on the refectory wall of Santa Maria delle Grazie, this mural depicts the moment Jesus announces a betrayal. Its innovative composition captures the disciples’ emotions and is widely regarded as one of the most important works of the Renaissance. The painting and convent are UNESCO World Heritage sites, highlighting their universal value.
